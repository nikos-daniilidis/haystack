Trends in Physical Review Letters¶

The purpose of this notebook is to explore the historic evolution of trends in physics, as revealed by the Physical Review Letters archive. Physical Review Letters was introduced in 1958 by the American Physical Society as a means to publish results in a quick 'n dirty manner. The idea was that some results are important enough to warrant a quick few-page report before a full length report could be written. Over the years, PRL became the standard for physics research, with every result which is supposed to be somehow worth learning about appearing in it (if not in Nature or Science). At the same time, the results which appeared in PRL were guaranteed high enough impact, that the full-length reports were often not published. I might return to the effects of impactism on modern research later, but now I am just thirsty to look at research trends.

Plot the trends for different categories¶

So, it is finally time to plot the trends. You might have noticed that there are a total of 24 different categories, which seems redundant. The reason for this is that as research evolved, old titles became obsolete, some categories had to be split into two, and in the process of doing this some categories which nnever caught on appeared and disappeared within a number of years.

To figure out what's going on, we'll plot selections of the existing categories. The hope is that at the end of this process we will manage to regroup the categories in a compact and meaningful way.

def plot_columns(df, col_list, labl_list, x_labl='', y_labl='', ttle='', linestyle_dict = linestyledict,logarithmic=False):
    '''
    For a selected column out of a dataframe, drop the zero entries, in column trm 
    and plot the entries under trm vs their index.
    Input
    ------------
        df: a dataframe of the form
                    | term1 | term2 | term3 ...
        publication |
        year        |
            year1   |  f11  |  f12  |  f13  ...
            year2   |  f21  |  f22  |  f23  ...
        col_list: a list of strings, specifying terms for which I want to make plot
        labl_list: list of strings, specifying the labels to put for each term on the plot
        topic: a string describing the topic, to put on the title of the plot
    '''
    for nn,trm in enumerate(col_list):
        flist = df.ix[:,trm].tolist()
        ilist = df.index.values.tolist()
        zero_indices = [i for i, e in enumerate(flist) if e == 0]
        #print max(zero_indices)
        cnt = 0
        for ind in zero_indices:
            try:
                del flist[ind-cnt]
                del ilist[ind-cnt]
                cnt+=1
            except:
                print len(flist)
                print len(ilist)
                print ind
        line = linestyle_dict[trm][0]+linestyle_dict[trm][1]
        plt.plot(ilist , flist, line, alpha=0.6 , label=labl_list[nn])
    plt.axis([1970, 2010, 0., 0.7])
    plt.legend(bbox_to_anchor=(1.05, 1), loc=2, borderaxespad=0.)
    plt.title(ttle)
    plt.xlabel(x_labl)
    plt.ylabel(y_labl)
    plt.show()

There seems to be a depression in plasmas/fluids/etc between 1973 and 1992, but this is likely due to a 'wandering unlabeled scholar effect', where areas of research do not clearly fall within any of the existing categories. During this period, the area of 'General physics' is having a period of increased activity -likely due to the fact that many groups which did not publish under 'Fluids, Plasmas, and Electric Discharges', nor under 'Classical Phenomenology' found a better fit under the 'General physics' label.

Such instances highlight the Editor's classification dilemma: How to pick classification categories which will include most of the work without complains from the authors, but also last a long time. This is a hard rpoblem, which explains why terms like General Physics persist, but occasionally acquire subclasses ('quantum information' and 'biological physics'). Such subclasses will either take on a life of their own (once a field gets established), or perish.

The End¶

This has been an interesting exercise, and taught me things I did not know about physics. At the same time, it was a clear demonstration that if I decide to look closer into the categories and clusters of scientific research, I will have to do it using hierarchical clustering algorithms, be aware of the limmitations of the pre-assigned labels. I will need to use my best judgement in choosing/reducing among the existing category names, but always keep in mind that some of the labels were poorly chosen, or are simply too generic to show much of a consistent pattern. For example, what internal consistency can I expect to find in areas like 'General Physics', which serve as containers for the emerging and uncategorizable areas of the day?
